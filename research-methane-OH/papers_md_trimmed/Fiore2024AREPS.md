# Abstract

The hydroxyl radical (OH) largely controls the tropospheric self-cleansing capacity by reacting with gases harmful to the environment and human health. OH concentrations are determined locally by competing production and loss processes. Lacking strong observational constraints, models differ in how they balance these processes,such that the sign of past and future OH changes is uncertain. In a warmer climate, OH production will increase due to its water vapor dependence, partially offset by faster OH-methane loss. Weather-sensitive emissions will also likely increase,although their net im-pact on global mean OH depends on the balance between source (nitrogen oxides) and sink (reactive carbon) gases. Lightning activity increases OH, but its response to climate warming is of uncertain sign. To enable confident projections of OH, we recommend efforts to reduce uncertainties in kinetic reactions, in measured and modeled OH, in proxies for past OH concentrations, and in source and sink gas emissions.

OH is strongly modulated by internal climate variability despite its life-time ofa few seconds at most, with implications for interpreting trends in methane.

■ Improved kinetic constraints on key reactions would strengthen confidence in regional and global OH budgets,and in the response of OH to climate change. Future OH changes will depend on uncertain and compensating processes involving weather-sensitive chemistry and emissions, plus human choices. Technological solutions to climate change willikely impact tropospheric oxidizing capacity and merit further study prior to implementation.

# 1. INTRODUCTION

Earth's climate and air quality are strongly coupled to oxidizing photochemical reactions in the troposphere. Tropospheric photochemistry continuously removes reduced gases,as has ocurred since oxygen emerged as a major atmospheric constituent. The term atmospheric oxidizing ca-pacity refers to the abundance of major atmospheric oxidants (Thompson 1992). Many challenges existin quantifying recent trends and variations in this atmospheric cleansing service.For example, it is not possble to directly observe the response of the tropospheric oxidizing capacity to natural climatic fluctuations and to anthropogenic climate change. Current knowledge is thus largely based on proxy evidence.

The hydroxyl radical(OH) (see the sidebar titled Definitions ofChemical Species and Families) is the major tropospheric atmospheric oxidant (Levy 1971) and serves as the focal point for this review. Reaction with OH oxidizes many reduced carbon gases to carbon dioxide $\mathrm { ( C O } _ { 2 } \mathrm { ) }$ and water; such gases include air pollutants, greenhouse gases,and stratospheric ozone-depleting substances. As the primary loss pathway for methane,OH sets the atmospheric lifetime of this greenhouse gas that ranks second behind $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ in terms of radiative forcing between the preindustrial and present day (IPCC 2023). Oxidation by OH also sets the lifetime of carbon monoxide (CO),an air pollutant that is a direct product of incomplete combustion and an intermediate produced during the oxidation of methane and many other volatile organic compounds (VOCs).The lifetime of OH is less than a few seconds (Lelieveld et al.2016),rendering a global observing system impractical if not impossible.Direct measurements of OH,common during field campaigns (Stone et al. 2012), enable process-level evaluations of models (Nicely et al.2016).

Reactions with CO and methane are key sinks for OHat the global scale,triggering nonlinear chemical feedbacks that cascade acros time and space by linking the OH radical with methane and its decadal lifetime (Prather 1996). Complex chemical couplings also occur during the production of OH, which is inextricably tied to tropospheric ozone (Figure 1),an air pollutant damaging to human health and vegetation that ranks as the third most important anthropogenic greenhouse gas after $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and methane in terms of radiative forcing from the preindustrial to the present day (IPCC 2023). Primary production of OH,as defined here,requires ozone,ultraviolet radiation, and water vapor.A smallr direct source of OH involves photolysis of VOCs.This OH then initi-ates the oxidation of VOCs or CO,which produces tropospheric ozone in the presence of nitrogen oxides $\mathrm { ( N O } _ { x } )$ ,dwarfing ozone transported from the stratosphere by an order of magnitude while also recycling OH.

Much effort has been expended to quantify and interpret recent decadal trends in methane, but variations due to its sink with OH pose a major uncertainty (Turner et al. 2O19).Long-term trends and interannual variability in global mean OH are inferred through inverse methods constrained by observations of methyl chloroform (Lovelock 1977,Montzka et al.2011, Patra et al. 2021, Prinn et al.1992),a species primarily lost through reaction with OH. Knowledge of the OH budget globaly is largely based on atmospheric chemistry models that include photochemical reactions measured in lab studies (e.g., Burkholder et al.2O19)or in some cases based on theory,and are evaluated with field measurements (e.g.,Miller & Brune 2022). Global atmospheric chemistry models disagree as to the importance of diferent OH production and loss terms (Wild et al. 2O2O). Advancing understanding requires observational evidence that can discriminate across models and test their process representation. A typical approach to model evaluation uses field observations of the species involved in OH production and loss to constrain photochemical box models.These studies are limited to relatively smal regions and time periods covered by field campaigns (Stone et al.2012), which sample the local processes over which the global mean integrates. A synthesis of two decades of field campaigns with varying environmen-tal and chemical conditions found that simulations of free tropospheric OH generally agree with observations within the measurement uncertainty, while pointing to a need for further study of regions with plentiful reactive carbon compounds (Miller & Brune 2O22). Long-term measurement sites have also yielded foundational, process-based insights into OH drivers (e.g.,Rohrer & Berresheim 2O06).(For definitions of the chemical species discussed below,see the sidebar titled Definitions of Chemical Species and Families.)

# 2.2.Decadal Trends

Decadal trends in OH, inferred from inverse modeling approaches constrained by long-lived gases that react with OH, or simulated with forward atmospheric chemistry models,are reviewed elsewhere (Chua et al.2023, Szopa et al.2021, Turner et al. 2019).We focus here on studies applying models to parse individual budget terms contributing to decadal trends in OH. Chua et al.(2023) find a $1 4 \%$ increase in total OH production and loss,and a $5 \%$ increase in global annual airmass-weighted OH(Lawrence et al. 2001) during 1980-2014,agreeing with the sign of simulated changes in other models (Stevenson et al. 2O20, Turner et al.2017). They attribute this increase to larger production driven by the concomitant rise in anthropogenic $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ emissions and methane concentrations,which together increase ozone and associated primary production plus recycling via $\mathrm { H O } _ { 2 } + \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ and $\mathrm { H O } _ { 2 } + \mathrm { N O } .$ Over a similar time period (1980-2010), Zhao et al.(2o2O) find a multi-model average increase of ${ \sim } 9 \%$ in total OH production [sum of $\mathrm { O ^ { 1 } D + H _ { 2 } O \ ( + 5 \% ) }$ ， $\mathrm { N O } + \mathrm { H O } _ { 2 }$ $( + 3 \% )$ ,and $\mathrm { O } _ { 3 } + \mathrm { H O } _ { 2 } \ : ( + 1 \% ) ]$ after interannual variability is removed. # 2.3. Spatial Variations

Observations of well-mixed methyl chloroform and other proposed long-lived proxies for OH (Liang et al. 2017) constrain global annual mean OH (Patra et al. 2021) but cannot map spatial variations in OH beyond the interhemispheric gradient (Patra et al.2O14).Measurements of $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C O }$ may offer new constraints on spatial and seasonal variability in OH (Petrenko et al. 2021). Spivakovsky et al. (2Ooo) developed a global-scale seasonal OH climatology by constraining a box model with measurements of OH sources and sinks.It has been used for more than two decades to benchmark global model simulations (e.g., Zhao et al. 2019). We briefly review promising applications of newly available satelite retrievals and airborne observations to map spatial patterns in OH.

Pioneering applications of satellite data show promise for mapping spatial variations in total or partial tropospheric columns of OH. Some studies use species that mirror OH production or loss under certain conditions: methane(Zhang etal.2018),formaldehyde (HCHO)(Wolfe etal.2019), and HCHO together with isoprene (Wells et al. 2020),although in situ sampling suggests large local variations may limit accuracy (Guo et al.2023). Some studies use multiple chemical species retrieved from satellite to infer OH variations.For example,a machine learning approach reveals OH trends ranging from $- 1 7 \%$ to $+ 1 1 \%$ in different North American cities due to anthropogenic emission changes (Zhu et al.2022).A simplified steady-state model for OH production and loss links interannual variability of tropical lower tropospheric OH (between $- 7 \%$ and $+ 8 \%$ to the influences of ozone on primary production and of CO on OH loss (Pimlott et al.2022). Satellite HCHO columns show an order-of-magnitude depression in OH over the tropical Pacific Warm Pool (Wolfe et al. 2019) where field measurements suggest a strong sensitivity to $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ (Nicely et al. 2016).

# 2.4. Uncertainties

Knowledge gaps in the processes controlling OH concentrations are particularly large in some atmospheric regions. Upper tropospheric model-measurement discrepancies (Brune et al.2020) may reflect a missing $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ source (Dang et al.2023) and/or errors in N ${ \mathrm { \small { [ O - N O _ { 2 } - O _ { 3 } } } }$ photochemistry (Silvern et al.2O18).Evaluation of current global models over the remote marine troposphere shows that they underestimate ozone production below $2 ~ \mathrm { k m }$ where $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ concentrations are too low (Guo et al. 2O23),implying errors in OH recycling.Models also underestimate near-surface OH reactivity, possibly associated with oceanic emissions of organic gases (Thames et al. 2020, Travis et al. 202O). Other field measurements confirm known chemistry in the clean remote marine boundary layer but diverge in air masses containing aged pollution,implying the presence of additional $\mathrm { R O } _ { 2 }$ (Andersen et al.2022). Overall,observational constraints on $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ distributions would improve modeling of spatial variations in OH (Fiore et al.20o8,Labrador et al.2004).

# 3. SENSITIVITY OF OH TO FACTORS MODULATED BY CLIMATE

In the modern atmosphere,global mean OHis sensitive to a limited setof factors (Section 2): ratios of emissions (or abundances) of reactive nitrogen and carbon (Wang & Jacob 1998), water vapor, tropospheric ozone,and ozone photolysis rates $[ \mathrm { J O } _ { 3 }  \mathrm { O } ^ { 1 } \mathrm { D }$ ,which in turn depend strongly on overhead $\mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ columns (e.g.,Murray et al.2014)]. Below, we summarize processes through which OH production and loss may respond to climate change.

# 3.1. Temperature-Sensitive Chemistry

One of the most robust relationships between climate variations and OH involves water vapor.Atmospheric water vapor increases by $7 \%$ per $1 \mathrm { \textrm { K } }$ warming of global annual mean surface temperature (e.g.,Held & Soden 20o6).Where primary OH production is limited by the avail-ability of water vapor, such as in the dry middle-to-upper troposphere,the overall $\mathrm { H O } _ { x }$ supply will increase as the troposphere warms.A reduced-form model analysis over the remote marine troposphere attributes observed OH spatial variations to fluctuations in measured $_ \mathrm { H } \mathrm { _ 2 O }$ (Baublitz et al.2023).Analysis of global models with field measurements points to a role for water vapor in driving intermodel differences in OH (Prather et al.2O18).Further work is needed to determine whether drivers of spatial variations deduced from field campaigns also control interannual variability in global mean OH. The increase in water vapor with temperature is likely the dominant response of OH production in a warmer climate (e.g.,Johnson et al.1999).

We consider the temperature dependence of several key chemical pathways through which OH is produced or lost (Table 1).We contrast rate coefcients for a warm near-surface $( 3 0 0 \mathrm { K } )$ versus free tropospheric $( 2 5 0 \mathrm { K } )$ airmass and for a $+ 5 \mathrm { K }$ increase in tropospheric mean temperature from 250 to $2 5 5 \mathrm { K } ,$ ,within the range of model projections for future climate change (IPCC 2O23). The most temperature-sensitive loss rate coefficient is $k _ { \mathrm { C H 4 + O H } }$ ,which increases by $1 4 \%$ and more than $3 0 0 \%$ for a 5 versus $5 0 ~ \mathrm { K }$ temperature increase (Table 1). This temperature sensitivity explains why the lifetime of methane and its response to climate change depend on how OH willchange in the warm tropical lower troposphere.In contrast, the primary loss pathway for OH,reaction with CO, has little dependence on temperature (Table 1). While most major OH production pathways are not strongly sensitive to temperature, $k _ { \mathrm { H O 2 + O 3 } }$ proceeds $4 \%$ and $3 9 \%$ faster for increases of $+ 5$ and $+ 5 0 \mathrm { K }$ relative to $2 5 0 \mathrm { K }$ ,and $k _ { \mathrm { N O + H O 2 } }$ and $k _ { \mathrm { N O + C H 3 O 2 } }$ proceed $2 \%$ and more than $1 5 \%$ more slowly,respectively. While tropospheric warming could thus lead to smal regional shifts in the balance of the recycling pathways shown in Figure 2,none of the changes for a $+ 5 \mathrm { K }$ tropospheric warming exceed the uncertainty in the reaction kinetics,highlighting the importance of continued laboratory efforts to narrow this uncertainty (Table 1).

# LIGHTNING $\mathbf { H O } _ { x }$

Lightning storms have long been hypothesized to produce $\mathrm { H O } _ { x }$ , in addition to $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ (Section 3.2), but early studies suggested that the lifetime oflightning $\mathrm { H O } _ { x }$ was too short to influence global oxidative capacity (Bhetanabhotla et al. 1985). Recent aircraft measurements,however, show thatlightning storms generate OHabundances several orders of magnitude greater than previously thought, potentially contributing $2 - 1 6 \%$ of global OH oxidation capacity (Brune et al.2O21). In addition to visible lightning,electrified storms contain weaker electrical discharges, such as streamers and corona (Kostinskiy etal. 2O20),some of which are subvisible.These weaker discharges generate litle heating but produce ultravioletradiation or electrons with sufcient energy to dissociate both water vapor and molecular oxygen,leading immediately to production ofOHand ozone (Jenkins et al.2021).A model studysuggests that a direct lightning source of OH could enhance mass-weighted global OH by $0 . 3 { - } 1 0 \%$ , with the large range of uncertainty indicating the need for further process-based observational constraints (Mao et al.2021). Quantifying the ozone produced in electrical storms is dificult because it cannot easily be distinguished from the ozone-rich stratospheric air that is pulled into the troposphere during these storms (Brune et al. 2021).

Lightning $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ is one of the strongest levers by which OH increases globally, given the lack of coemited species serving as OH sinks (Murray et al. 2013)(Figure 5). New evidence sug-gests lightning may substantially boost the atmospheric oxidizing capacity by producing $\mathrm { H O } _ { x }$ (see the sidebar titled Lightning $\mathrm { H O } _ { x } ,$ ). The extremely high temperatures that characterize lightning flashes initiate a series of reactions leading to large quantities of $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ (Murray 2016,Navarro-Gonzalez et al. 2001,Pickering et al. 1990).Lightning $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ enhances ozone globally with tropical lightning influencing the chemical composition even over mid-latitudes (Mickley et al. 2001). Global atmospheric chemistry-climate models commonly parameterize this source as a powerlaw dependence to convective cloud top heights, which usually, though not always,increases with climate warming $[ - 0 . 0 3$ to $0 . 6 1 \mathrm { T g } ( \mathrm { N } )$ year/K(Thornhill et al.2021)],or about $1 0 \%$ globally for every $+ 1$ K (Price 2Oo9).A parameterization based on upward cloud ice flux,linked more closely to microphysics, instead suggests climate warming decreases the global flash rate and associated $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ source (Finney et al. 2018) $[ 1 5 \%$ decrease under a strong warming [representative concentration pathway (RCP)8.5] scenario]. In a cloud-resolving model, Romps (2019) probes different proxies for flash rates, including a scaling for lightning flashes based on convective available potential energy and precipitation,but is unable to conclusively determine the sign of the lightning response to climate change, which is likely to vary by region (e.g., Chakraborty et al.2O21, Chen et al.2021,Kahraman et al. 2022).

Overall, biogenic sources are expected to increase in a warmer climate (Figure 5). These sources include methane produced by methanogens in wetlands,BVOC emitted from vegetation, and NO produced during microbial nitrification or denitrification within soils.A positive relationship between $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and wetland methane emissions is supported by ice core records (Petit et al. 1999),although the magnitude of this relationship is uncertain and model dependent (Melton et al. 2013).Process-based wetland models tend to simulate declining tropical methane emissions with higher temperatures and increasing global emissons with precipitation, but these dependencies are uncertain (Melton et al.2O13). Climate-driven changes in wetland emissons are neglected in most chemistry-climate multi-model studies to date, but process-based wetland models suggest increases of $3 3 \mathrm { - } 6 0 \%$ by 2100 (Dean et al.2018),with two estimates from chemistry-climate models indicating 20 or $6 0 \mathrm { T g } \mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ year/K(Thornhill et al.2021).Additional wetland methane emissions with rising $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ would substantially enhance atmospheric methane (Kleinen et al.2021), increasing the OH sink.

Chemistry-climate models that allow BVOC emissions to respond to meteorology (Guenther et al. 2012) generally project future increases with temperature [e.g., $3 0 { - } 2 3 0 \mathrm { T g }$ year/K (Thornhill et al.2021)] and thus increase the OH sink (Figure 5). While rising atmospheric $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ may offset some of the expected temperature-driven increase in isoprene (the most abundant individual BVOC),the magnitude of this effect may be lessened as temperatures rise (Sharkey & Monson 2014). Soil NO emissions also increase with temperature,and pulsing occurs following precipitation on dry soils (Hudman et al. 2O12).Increasing soil $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ with global warming would enhance OH and ozone (Figure 5). Higher sea surface temperatures under climate change may increase the flux of marine halogens, produced by either biochemical or abiotic processes in the ocean into the atmosphere in some regions,with uncertain consequences for ozone and in turn OH (Iglesias-Suarez et al. 2020).

Wildfires are strongly sensitive to rising temperature and drier conditions (Abatzoglou & Williams 2O16),and climate change is expected to increase wildfire activity in many regions (Jones et al. 2022).All of the gases in Figure 5 are emitted by fires and serve as both sources and sinks of OH. Observations provide evidence for OH production locally within smoke plumes (Theys et al.2020),but models suggest that the sink gases dominate the net impact on OH in the present global atmosphere (Murray et al. 2O13) and that a doubling of wildfire emissons could decrease global mean OH by about $6 \%$ (Mao et al. 2013b).

Anthropogenic emissions are usually assumed to have little sensitivity to temperature relative to the other sources discussed above (Figure 5). Some anthropogenic emissions increase on warmer days, including evaporative NMVOC and agricultural emissons.In some regions,power plant emissions also increase with cooling demand on warmer days (Abel et al.2O17).Independent of climate change,continued increases in anthropogenic $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ from aircraft and ships will increase OH (Butler et al.2020, Terrenoire et al.2022),although estimated OH increases may be biased high due to poor resolution of plume chemistry in global models (Eastham & Jacob 2017). Given their competing influences on production and loss pathways,the netinfluence of weather-sensitive emissions on OHis highly uncertain.For example,if lightning $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ were to decline while wetland methane emissions increased, then the global OH abundance would decrease in response.

# 3.3. Climate Variability and Possible Dynamical Changes

Even though locally at a steady state, OH concentrations fluctuate as internal climate variability (Lehner & Deser 2O23) causes variations in OH production and loss pathways through the processes discussed above.Figure 6 illustrates that $5 - 1 0 \%$ variations (one standard deviation of the range across an ensemble generated with a single model) in annual mean tropospheric column OH can occur solely because of climate variability.This range is similar to year-to-year variability of generally less than $\pm 5 \%$ simulated by other global atmospheric chemistry models (Szopa et al. 2021), with variations as high as $2 \%$ linked to the net effects of meteorology and biomass burning alone (Chua et al. 2O23). Two studies suggest that interannual OH variability has weakened in the early twenty-first century versus the last few decades of the twentieth century (He et al.2020, Zhao et al.2019). Murray et al. (2013; see their table 2) quantify the sensitivities of interannual variability in global annual mean OH to specific production and loss pathways,and they highlight a strong sensitivity of OH to lightning $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ over the 1998-2006 period.

El Nino-Southern Oscillation (ENSO) is the dominant mode of naturally arising climate variability (Lee et al. 2O21) and is known to influence global OH. This strong regional signal in the tropical Pacific is evident in Figure 6.During the boreal winter season,ENSO modulates approximately $3 0 \%$ of the total variance in tropospheric column and upper tropospheric OH(Anderson et al. 2021). This influence occurs through multiple production and loss pathways involving water vapor and tropospheric ozone (Chandra et al. 1998, Ziemke et al.2010),lightning $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ (Turner et al. 2018),and biomass burning (Duncan et al. 2003). Regional OH responses occur through atmospheric teleconnections involving changes in upper tropospheric $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ and lower tropospheric primary production (Anderson et al. 2O21). Models indicate net negative OH anomalies during El Nino events due to biomass burning (e.g., Zhao et al.2O2O), with tropospheric column OH as much as $1 0 { - } 1 5 \%$ lower over the tropical Pacific and Australia (Anderson et al.2O21).Higher OH during La Nina has been attributed to enhanced lightning $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ (Anderson et al. 2021, Turner et al. 2018). Despite the lack of consensus on future changes in ENSO intensity or variability, ENSO will continue to dominate interannual variability (Lee et al. 2O21),amplifying (e.g., the 2023 El Nino) or offsetting the overall upward warming trend in any given year. This variabil-ity will tus continue to influence OH globally and should be accounted for when interpreting observed trends in OH as well as the trace gases whose lifetimes are set by OH.

Climate change may also influence OH by changing atmospheric circulation. As water vapor rises,convective mass flux decreases (Held & Soden 2O06), potentially altering lightning $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ and the vertical mixing of OH source and sink gases. An expanding tropical area under climate warming may increase primary production of OH (Nicely et al.2018). # 4. PREINDUSTRIAL AND PALEO OXIDATION CAPACITY

Gases and aerosols trapped in ice cores in polar regions or high-altitude tropical glaciers can serve as proxies to reconstruct concentrations of oxidative gases in preindustrial and paleo atmospheres (Alexander & Mickley 2015).Lessons learned from the past can be used to constrain model efforts of future atmospheres.For example, knowledge of how the lower temperatures during the last glacial period afected the flux of stratospheric ozone into the troposphere would increase confidence in model simulations of future changes in this flux (Wang et al. 2O22).Most oxidants, including OH,are not preserved directly in ice cores.In this section, we describe proxies for past oxidants and discuss modeling of preindustrial and paleo atmospheres.Following Hawkins et al. (2017),we define the preindustrial era from 172O to 1800; the last glacial period extends from 115,000 to 11,700 years ago.

# 4.1. Paleo and Preindustrial Observations

Hydrogen peroxide $( \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ or HOOH) is the only oxidative component of the atmosphere that has been directly measured in ice cores.HOOH is a reservoir species for $\mathrm { H O } _ { x }$ (Figure 2), so its abundance provides clues on past OH variations (Anderson et al. 2O22),especially in combination with models (e.g.,Murray et al.2O14). The Antarctic ice core record indicates at least a $5 0 \%$ increase in HOOH since 1900, with most of the increase occurring since 197o (Lamarque et al. 2011).Although ice core measurements of HOOH exist as far back as 150o (Frey et al. 2006), interpretation of these records is complicated by chemical processing of this species within the ice.Nonetheless,the recent 1900-2000 increase in observed HOOH is consistent with increasing tropospheric ozone.In addition,loss of stratospheric ozone over Antarctica may have allowed more ultraviolet sunlight to penetrate the troposphere and accelerate ozone photolysis,increasing OH and HOOH (Lamarque et al. 2011).

Three other gases preserved in ice cores may indirectly shed light on the oxidative capacity of the atmosphere: HCHO, CO,and methane.HCHO is a product of the oxidation of NMVOCs and methane, which can serve as an OH sink or instead photolyze to generate OH(Bates & Jacob 2019) (Figure 2). Like HOOH, HCHO can undergo postdepositional chemistry in ice,and so its ice core record must be interpreted with caution (Hutterli et al.2Oo04)although measurements suggest an increase since the preindustrial era.Increasing CO and methane,both sinks for OH (Figure 2),are expected to decrease OH. CO may also be affected by in situ chemical processing in ice,and its variations may reflect the decomposition of organic material,especially compounds emitted during biomass burning (Fain et al.2014).

In contrast to HCHO and CO,methane is stable in ice,and trends in ice core records closely match those in the atmosphere over time.These records reveal low levels of methane during the last glacial period compared to the preindustrial era,likely due to a reduction in wetland areas (Rhodes et al.2O17).During the cold,dry glacial period, wetlands in the tropics shrank while in boreal regions polar ice caps covered wetlands (Kaplan etal.2Oo6).During the Holocene, wetlands expanded in size and methane levels rose.Since the 18oOs,methane has more than doubled,reflecting oil and gas systems,agricultural activity,and mining(e.g.,Turner et al.2O16).As methane and the CO produced during its oxidation are sinks of OH,interpreting the long-term record of methane in ice can yield insights into past OH levels.

Isotopic signatures in ice core components also contain information on oxidation capacity in past atmospheres.Such components include molecular oxygen, methane,CO,and sulfate and nitrate aerosol.For example,studies have relied on an isotopic quantity, $\Delta ^ { 1 7 } \mathrm { O } ( \mathrm { S O } _ { 4 } { } ^ { 2 - } )$ , to probe oxidation processes in the last glacial period. # 4.2. Models of Paleo and Preindustrial OH

Three-dimensional chemical transport models have been deployed to recreate the oxidation capacity of past atmospheres and to interpret the observations described in Section 4.1.Some of these models feature fuly interactive chemistry-climate capability, in which modeled changes in aerosols and greenhouse gases affect meteorology, which in turn influences atmospheric composition.Multi-model comparisons reveal litle consensus on changes in OH abundance since the last glacial period or even since the preindustrial era (Alexander & Mickley 2015) (Figure 4).

In past atmospheres, models suggest that global mean OH was most sensitive to lightning $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ emissions, water vapor,and the density of the stratospheric ozone layer(Murray et al.2O13,2014). Trends in biomass burning likely had relatively little effect,as fire emissions include both nitrogen and carbon species, with opposing effects on OH abundance (Figure 5). During the last glacial period, models agree that lower global temperatures and water vapor likely depressed OH levels, with some compensation possible from lower biogenic emissions (Achakulwisut et al.2015, Valdes et al. 2005).Figure 4 shows that models differ not just in magnitude but also in the sign of their simulated OH trend from the preindustrial to the present. Some of this intermodel diversity is tied to different ratios in the global mean tropospheric CO to $\mathrm { N O } _ { x }$ burdens (Naik et al.2013).

# 5.OH IN THE TWENTY-FIRST CENTURY

Projection of OH into the twenty-first century is challenging due to an array of uncertainties that can be classified into three major categories (Hawkins & Sutton 20o9).First, internal variability, inherent in the climate system,is an irreducible source of uncertainty (Deser et al.2O12,Lehner & Deser 2023) (Figure 6b). Second, model (structural) uncertainty is potentially reducible as knowledge advances and model representation of the governing processes improves (Hawkins & Sutton 2009).At present, intermodel differences (Figure 4) indicate insufficient knowledge of how OH will respond to changing meteorology and how different chemical and meteorological feedbacks on oxidation operate.Third,scenario uncertainty reflects the collective but unknown choices by individuals and governments and the resulting anthropogenic emissions that yield different oxida-tive capacities (Figure 4).For example,societal choices regarding energy and agricultural systems will affect OH source and sink gases (Figure 5),and thereby the balance among OH production and loss pathways (Figures 2 and 3).

Many atmospheric chemistry models forced with future climate and air pollutant emission scenarios project global annual mean OH changes by 21Oo that are similar to within $2 0 \%$ ,with the sign dependent both on the scenario and on the representation of model processes (Murray et al.2021,Voulgarakis et al.2013,Wild et al.2020). Models robustly indicate that climate change alone will increase OH due to rising water vapor, which together with the strong temperature sensitivity of $k _ { \mathrm { C H 4 + O H } }$ (Table 1) leads to a negative climate feedback by shortening the methane lifetime against OH by $0 . 3 3 \pm 0 . 1$ year/K (Voulgarakis et al.2013).From the perspective of OH, the $k _ { \mathrm { C H 4 + O H } }$ temperature sensitivity bufers OH increases from rising water vapor (John et al. 2012).This increase in the OH sink is amplified by the feedback of methane on its own lifetime because methane and the CO produced during its oxidation are major OH sinks (Prather 1996, Holmes 2018) (Section 2.1).T date,most chemistry-climate models do not include increases in methane emissions from wetlands in response to rising $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ (Section 3.2),a phenomenon that would further deplete OH. The net balance of these climate-sensitive processes also depends on future stratospheric ozone concentrations,which wil respond to both climate change and human-driven emissions of ozone depleting substances.In one model, instantaneous cessation of all anthropogenic methane emissons relative to a future scenario [socio-economic pathway (SSP) 3-7.O] decreases surface methane concentrations by more than $3 0 \%$ to below preindustrial concentrations,attributed partially to increased water vapor abundances (Staniaszek et al. 2022). Models,however, difer in both their simulations of water vapor (Prather et al.2O18)and OH sensitivity to water vapor (Wild et al.2020).

# 5.1. Technological Wildcards

Novel technologies emerging during the twenty-first century intended to mitigate climate change could perturb atmospheric OH,and we briefly provide a few examples here as summarized in Table 3. Hydrogen $\left( \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \right)$ has been proposed as an alternative energy source to fossil fuel. $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 }$ burns cleanly, without carbon by-products that lead to the greenhouse gas $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ .However, the production of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 }$ may involve significant methane emissions (Bertagni et al.2O22,Ocko & Hamburg 2022). Once produced, $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 }$ can easily escape from pipelines and other infrastructure to the ambient atmosphere and compete with methane for reaction with OH (Equation 2). A longer methane lifetime would increase climate warming,as would the $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 }$ -mediated increase in ozone production and stratospheric water vapor (Ocko & Hamburg 2O22,Paulot et al.2021).

Proposed initiatives for solar geoengineering could also alter tropospheric OH, with the sign dependent on the method. Injecting a layer of aerosols into the stratosphere to reflect incoming sunlight back to space and thus cool the planet (Irvine et al.2019,MacMartin et al.2019) could, depending on aerosol composition,diminish the abundance of stratospheric ozone through heterogeneous reactions (Pitari et al.2O14). The resulting increase in the flux of ultraviolet sunlight into the troposphere could ramp up OH primary production by increasing photolysis rates of tro-pospheric ozone.A typical scenario for stratospheric aerosol injection could increase global mean OH in the troposphere by as much as $9 \%$ (Moch et al. 2023).

# SUMMARY POINTS

1. Tropospheric concentrations of the hydroxyl radical (OH) set the atmospheric life-times of some greenhouse gases (methane, ozone), air pollutants (CO, volatile organic compounds)，and replacements for stratospheric ozone-depleting substances (hydrofluorocarbons and hydrochlorofluorocarbons), tying our capacity to predict future environmental change to knowledge of the processes controlling OH (Figure 1).   
2. The most strongly temperature-sensitive chemical processes controlling OH production and loss are the dependence of OH primary production on water vapor and the kinetic rate coefficient for the methane-OH reaction (Table 1).   
3. Under climate change, emissions of OH source and sink gases are expected to increase but the net impact on OH depends on the balance of the source from nitrogen oxides versus reactive carbon OH sinks (Figure 5).   
4. Even with its atmospheric lifetime of a few seconds at most, OH is strongly modu-lated by internal climate variability, including through the El Nino-Southern Oscillation influence on meteorology and OH source and sink gases (Figure 6).   
5. While modeled and measured OH often agree,at least within their uncertainty ranges of $5 - 3 0 \%$ (Table 2), major knowledge gaps remain in the regional and global balances among OH production and loss pathways, most notably in the marine boundary layer and upper troposphere where model-measurement discrepancies are largest.

# FUTURE ISSUES

1. Future efforts should aim to reduce uncertainty in measurements, fundamental photoly-sis and reaction rates,and model process representation, including through coordinated laboratory,field,and modeling efforts. 2. Advances in observing systems and models wil enable insights into OH variations at subannual to interannual scales, the processes controlling these variations including OH reactivity across diferent atmospheric environments (e.g., urban, forested, clean, fire

influenced, remote), and their importance in setting the lifetimes of key atmospheric trace gases.   
3. Technological transitions proposed to mitigate climate change should account for OH and other oxidant responses to help avoid unintentional consequences.   
4. Efforts to interpret proxies of oxidative species from ice cores and reconstruct oxidizing capacities in paleo atmospheres can help to illuminate how the atmospheric oxidizing capacity wil respond to future climate change and to shifts in energy systems.   
5. Agricultural sources are growing in magnitude and in relative importance as other anthropogenic emissions decline, yet their net impact on oxidizing capacity is unclear.   
6. Additional lab and field studies are needed to constrain emerging knowledge of atmo-spheric chemistry, such as the role of aerosols in producing and destroying radicals, and of oxidation by halogens relative to OH.
